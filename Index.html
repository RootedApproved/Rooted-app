        What's a CSA?
        Short for Community Supported Agriculture. You pay a local farm in advance for a "share," and in return get a box of whatever's freshly harvested that week — usually picked up weekly from a set nearby location. It's a direct relationship with a real farm, not a subscription box company: you don't choose what's inside, you get what's in season, and your money supports that specific farm rather than a distributor.

        Standards & Tiers

        ROOTED is not a list of recommended products — it's the trusted standard for evaluating them. Every category is judged against its own published standard before a single product in it is rated. Standards come first. Products come second. People always.

        The Tier System

        Products sit in one of three tiers based on ROOTED's published standards. Every placement includes a written rationale. No pay-to-play. Ever.

        TIER 1Rooted Approved
        No meaningful compromises. Would confidently recommend to a family member.
        • Meets all primary ROOTED standards
        • Transparent sourcing and manufacturing
        • Third-party certification confirmed
        TIER 2Good Option
        Minor compromises — documented clearly. Still recommended for most people.
        • Meets most ROOTED standards
        • One or two minor flags — always disclosed
        TIER 3Budget / Transitional
        Acceptable when cost limits better options. Not ideal long-term.
        • Meets basic safety thresholds
        • Notable compromises — clearly disclosed
        The Rule

        No brand can pay to improve their tier. If a brand reformulates, we update the rating with a date stamp. Tier assignments are living documents.

        Category Standards

        Every category is evaluated against its own published standard before any product in it is assigned a tier. Tap a category to see exactly what ROOTED looks for, what raises or lowers confidence, and how Tier 1/2/3 is defined specifically for that category.

        Clothing (Men's)Live
        Clothing sits directly against skin for hours at a stretch. ROOTED's core concern is material honesty — what a garment is actually made of and treated with — plus durability, since a shirt that lasts eight years beats three that last one year each.
        Certified organic cotton, linen, hemp, or wool (GOTS, OEKO-TEX)Low-impact or natural dyes, disclosed dye processNo added synthetic performance finishes on natural fiberSynthetic fiber as the primary material, especially against skinPFAS-based water/stain repellent treatmentsUndisclosed or "proprietary" fabric treatments
        Read the full standard →
        What We're Optimizing For
        • Natural, breathable fiber content
        • Minimal, disclosed, non-toxic dye and finishing chemistry
        • Durability and garment lifespan
        • Manufacturing transparency
        Green Flags
        • Certified organic cotton, linen, hemp, or wool (GOTS, OEKO-TEX)
        • Low-impact or natural dyes, disclosed dye process
        • No added synthetic performance finishes on natural fiber
        • Reinforced, repairable construction
        • Disclosed factory location and labor practices
        Red Flags
        • Synthetic fiber as the primary material, especially against skin
        • PFAS-based water/stain repellent treatments
        • Undisclosed or "proprietary" fabric treatments
        • Formaldehyde-based wrinkle-resistant finishing
        • Fast-fashion construction signaling short intended lifespan
        Evaluation Questions
        • What is the fiber content, and is any of it certified organic?
        • Is the fabric treated with anything beyond dye?
        • Is the dye process disclosed, and is it low-impact or natural?
        • Will the construction hold up to years of regular wear and washing?
        • Is manufacturing origin and labor practice disclosed?
        Tradeoffs

        100% natural fiber vs. performance/stretch: A small percentage of elastane for fit (e.g., 3-5% in waistbands) is a reasonable, common compromise most people accept.

        Organic vs. conventional cotton: Conventional cotton isn't automatically disqualifying — dye/finishing chemistry matters more than the raw fiber growing method.

        Durability vs. price: A well-constructed garment at a higher price is usually the better long-run value; this is disclosed, not assumed.

        Tier Definitions

        Tier 1: Predominantly certified-organic or otherwise verified natural fiber, disclosed low-impact dye, no added synthetic performance finishes, durable construction, transparent manufacturing.

        Tier 2: Natural-fiber-dominant with a minor synthetic component, or natural fiber without organic certification but no red-flag finishing chemistry.

        Tier 3: Meets basic safety thresholds (no PFAS, no formaldehyde finishing) but may be majority synthetic blend or lack fiber/dye transparency.

        Exceptions
        • Technical/athletic gear with genuine performance synthetics may be evaluated on a separate, disclosed basis rather than penalized identically to everyday wear.
        • Budget constraints and regional availability are acknowledged — Tier 3 exists for this.
        Future Research Notes
        • Monitoring PFAS regulation changes affecting "performance" fabric treatments.
        • Emerging natural performance fibers (merino blends, lyocell) need a clearer green-flag threshold.
        • Microplastic shedding from synthetic-blend garments during washing is an active research area.
        Oral CareLive
        The mouth absorbs ingredients directly through mucous membranes, and oral care is used daily for life. ROOTED's concern is both formulation (what's actually in it) and the physical product itself, since toothbrushes and floss are frequently single-use plastic.
        Short, disclosed ingredient listsNatural abrasives and flavoring, not artificial sweeteners or dyesBiodegradable or compostable flossSodium lauryl sulfate (SLS) as a primary surfactantArtificial sweeteners or dyes with no functional purposeTriclosan or other persistent antibacterial agents
        Read the full standard →
        What We're Optimizing For
        • Ingredient simplicity and disclosed formulation
        • Avoidance of unnecessary synthetic additives
        • Material honesty in hard-goods
        • Reduced single-use plastic waste where a durable alternative performs equivalently
        Green Flags
        • Short, disclosed ingredient lists
        • Natural abrasives and flavoring, not artificial sweeteners or dyes
        • Biodegradable or compostable floss
        • Replaceable-head or durable-handle toothbrush designs
        • Independent lab testing disclosed
        Red Flags
        • Sodium lauryl sulfate (SLS) as a primary surfactant
        • Artificial sweeteners or dyes with no functional purpose
        • Triclosan or other persistent antibacterial agents
        • PTFE-coated or fully synthetic floss with no alternative offered
        • Fully disposable plastic toothbrush with no recycling/replacement program
        Evaluation Questions
        • Is the full ingredient list disclosed, and is it short relative to category norms?
        • Does the formula contain SLS, artificial dyes/sweeteners, or triclosan?
        • What is the product physically made from, and is any part single-use plastic with no better-designed alternative?
        • For fluoride products: is content and sourcing clearly disclosed, with a fluoride-free option also offered?
        • Is there independent testing behind any safety claim?
        Tradeoffs

        Fluoride vs. fluoride-free: ROOTED does not take a public position on fluoride's efficacy or safety — the standard is disclosure. Both fluoride and fluoride-free options are eligible for Tier 1 if otherwise compliant.

        Plastic-free vs. functional performance: A bamboo-handled brush with a nylon bristle head is a reasonable middle ground — full bristle "naturalness" isn't required.

        Tier Definitions

        Tier 1: Short, disclosed ingredient list free of SLS/artificial dyes/triclosan; durable or reduced-waste hard-goods design; fluoride content (if any) clearly disclosed.

        Tier 2: Minor formulation compromise or standard plastic hard-goods without a waste-reduction design, while avoiding all red-flag ingredients.

        Tier 3: Avoids the most significant red flags but may contain SLS or be a fully conventional disposable design.

        Exceptions
        • Orthodontic and retainer products prescribed by a dental professional are evaluated for material safety only, not penalized for lack of "natural" branding.
        • Travel-sized conventional products get the same standard as full-size.
        Future Research Notes
        • Microplastic exposure from toothbrush bristle wear — limited current data.
        • Tracking independent (non-industry-funded) research on SLS irritation prevalence.
        • Silicone-based interdental tools need a clearer green/red-flag position as more appear.
        Cookware & KitchenLive
        Cookware experiences repeated heating and direct food contact, which makes material choice unusually consequential — heat accelerates coating breakdown. Materials that don't need a coating to function (stainless, cast iron, carbon steel, glass) are structurally simpler to trust.
        Fully inert base material: stainless steel, cast iron, carbon steel, borosilicate glass, uncoated ceramicNo PTFE, PFOA, or other PFAS coatingsFully disclosed base material and coating compositionPTFE/PFAS non-stick coatings, regardless of "PFOA-free" marketingUndisclosed "proprietary" non-stick coating compositionPlastic components in direct hot-food contact
        Read the full standard →
        What We're Optimizing For
        • Food-contact material safety (inert vs. coated surfaces)
        • Durability and realistic lifetime value
        • Absence of unnecessary plastic in direct food contact
        • Honest heat-safety limits
        Green Flags
        • Fully inert base material: stainless steel, cast iron, carbon steel, borosilicate glass, uncoated ceramic
        • No PTFE, PFOA, or other PFAS coatings
        • Fully disclosed base material and coating composition
        • Wood, stainless, or silicone (not plastic) utensils for hot-food contact
        • Designed for multi-decade use with available replacement parts
        Red Flags
        • PTFE/PFAS non-stick coatings, regardless of "PFOA-free" marketing
        • Undisclosed "proprietary" non-stick coating composition
        • Plastic components in direct hot-food contact
        • Aluminum cookware with no anodized or sealed surface
        • Documented coating breakdown at normal cooking temperatures
        Evaluation Questions
        • What is the base material, and does it require a coating to function?
        • If coated, is the coating fully disclosed, and PFAS-free — not just "PFOA-free"?
        • What plastic, if any, touches food during normal use, and at what temperature?
        • Is the product designed and warrantied for long-term (10+ year) use?
        • Are there documented, independent reports of coating degradation under normal use?
        Tradeoffs

        Non-stick convenience vs. inert material: ROOTED does not currently consider mainstream non-stick coatings (PTFE or ceramic-based) Tier 1 — this is a real, conscious convenience tradeoff people make.

        Cast iron/carbon steel maintenance vs. stainless convenience: Not a safety tradeoff, just a usability one — noted, not scored.

        Weight/heat retention vs. ease of handling: Disclosed, not penalized.

        Tier Definitions

        Tier 1: Fully inert base material with no coating required for function, no plastic in hot-food contact, disclosed manufacturing.

        Tier 2: Inert base material with a minor plastic component not in food contact, or a fully disclosed ceramic-based non-stick coating with no PFAS.

        Tier 3: Conventional non-stick with disclosed "PFOA-free" PTFE coating and no documented early degradation.

        Exceptions
        • Professional/commercial kitchen equipment evaluated separately where a consumer standard doesn't apply.
        • Baby-feeding cookware/utensils held to a stricter version of this standard, not a looser one.
        Future Research Notes
        • Tracking PFAS regulatory action (several states have passed or proposed cookware-specific bans).
        • "Ceramic non-stick" covers genuinely different chemistries — needs clearer sub-classification.
        • Aluminum core (anodized, sandwiched) cookware needs more specific criteria.
        WaterLive
        Water is consumed constantly and in large volume, which makes even small per-serving differences meaningful over a lifetime. The standard spans two questions: what's filtered out of (or added to) the water, and what it sits in before reaching someone's mouth.
        NSF/ANSI-certified filtration for the specific contaminants claimed (42, 53, 58, or 401)Stainless steel, glass, or genuinely BPA/BPS/phthalate-free containersRemineralization disclosed for methods that strip mineralsFiltration claims with no independent certification behind themPolycarbonate (#7) plastic bottles or undisclosed "BPA-free" plasticReverse osmosis/distillation with no remineralization option
        Read the full standard →
        What We're Optimizing For
        • Verified contaminant reduction, not just marketing claims
        • Container material safety under heat or repeated reuse
        • Avoidance of unnecessary mineral stripping without remineralization
        • Realistic filter lifespan and maintenance disclosure
        Green Flags
        • NSF/ANSI-certified filtration for the specific contaminants claimed (42, 53, 58, or 401)
        • Stainless steel, glass, or genuinely BPA/BPS/phthalate-free containers
        • Remineralization disclosed for methods that strip minerals
        • Clear, published filter replacement schedule tied to real capacity testing
        Red Flags
        • Filtration claims with no independent certification behind them
        • Polycarbonate (#7) plastic bottles or undisclosed "BPA-free" plastic
        • Reverse osmosis/distillation with no remineralization option
        • Filter lifespan claims not backed by capacity testing data
        • Undisclosed bottled water sourcing
        Evaluation Questions
        • Is the filtration independently certified for the specific contaminants it claims?
        • What is the container/system built from, and is it stable under realistic heat/reuse?
        • If minerals are stripped, is remineralization addressed?
        • Is the maintenance schedule realistic and disclosed, not just marketed?
        • For bottled water, is source and treatment disclosed?
        Tradeoffs

        Filtration thoroughness vs. mineral content: Aggressive filtration removes more contaminants but also strips beneficial minerals — resolved by remineralization, not by picking a weaker filter.

        Convenience of bottled vs. environmental/plastic tradeoff: Not disqualified for convenience use, but disclosed as secondary to home filtration where available.

        Upfront system cost vs. long-term cost: A certified system usually costs less per gallon over time than routine bottled water.

        Tier Definitions

        Tier 1: Independently certified filtration for its specific claims, inert container material, remineralization addressed if applicable, realistic disclosed maintenance.

        Tier 2: Certified filtration with a minor gap, or a generally safe plastic container without full third-party verification.

        Tier 3: Basic, uncertified but reputable-brand filtration — meaningfully better than tap alone.

        Exceptions
        • Emergency preparedness water storage/purification evaluated against different, disclosed criteria (shelf stability, portability).
        • Regional water quality varies significantly; a product may be rated with a note on which contaminant profile it's most relevant for.
        Future Research Notes
        • PFAS-specific filtration certification is fast-moving; needs regular review.
        • Microplastic shedding from plastic filtration housings is an open research question.
        • Structured/hexagonal water claims currently lack independent scientific support.
        Personal CareLive
        Personal care products are applied directly to skin, often daily, and many ingredients are absorbed rather than just sitting on the surface. This is also the category most saturated with "clean beauty" marketing that doesn't correspond to real formulation differences.

        What We're Optimizing For
        • Ingredient simplicity and full disclosure
        • Avoidance of endocrine-disrupting or flagged synthetic ingredients

        • Packaging material safety for leave-on, high-contact products
        Green Flags

        • Recognizable, minimally processed ingredients where formulation allows
        • Relevant third-party certification (USDA Organic, EWG Verified, Leaping Bunny)
        • Mineral-based UV filters, disclosed nanoparticle status
        • Glass, aluminum, or verified-safe plastic packaging for leave-on products
        Red Flags

        • Talc without disclosed asbestos-testing verification
        Evaluation Questions
        • Is the full ingredient list disclosed, including what's inside "fragrance"?
        • Does the formula contain any flagged ingredient (parabens, phthalates, oxybenzone, etc.)?
        • Does it actually perform its stated function, based on independent evidence?
        • For sun protection: is UV filter type and nanoparticle status disclosed?
        • Is the packaging appropriate for a leave-on, repeated-use product?
        Tradeoffs

        "Clean" formulation vs. proven efficacy: ROOTED does not rate a poorly-performing natural product above a well-formulated conventional one purely on ingredient philosophy.

        Mineral vs. chemical sunscreen: Mineral is the green-flag default, but texture/cosmetic-elegance tradeoffs are real — a product someone won't wear provides zero protection.

        Antiperspirant function vs. ingredient simplicity: Blocking sweat requires an active ingredient; evaluated on its own safety merits.

        Tier Definitions

        Tier 1: Full ingredient transparency, no flagged ingredients, demonstrated efficacy, appropriate packaging.

        Tier 2: Mostly clean formulation with one minor flagged ingredient at low concentration and clear disclosure, or proven-effective conventional formula with a documented, minor tradeoff.

        Tier 3: Avoids the most significant red flags but may rely on undisclosed "fragrance" or lack third-party verification.

        Exceptions
        • Prescription or dermatologist-directed treatments evaluated for safety of inactive ingredients only — the active ingredient is a medical decision, outside ROOTED's scope.
        • Costume/theatrical or occasional-use cosmetics may be held to a lighter standard than daily-wear products.
        Future Research Notes
        • Tracking FDA/EU regulatory divergence on flagged ingredients.
        • Nanoparticle mineral sunscreen absorption research is still developing.
        • "Fragrance-free" vs. "unscented" needs clearer, consistent evaluation language.

        • Building a recurring re-verification cadence so listings don't silently go stale as the map grows.

        Read the full standard →
        What We're Optimizing For
        • Nutrient density relative to processing level
        • Ingredient list simplicity and recognizability
        • Sourcing transparency
        • Absence of unnecessary industrial additives
        Green Flags
        • Short ingredient lists of recognizable, whole-food ingredients
        • Verified organic, regenerative, or pasture-raised sourcing with named farms/regions
        • Minimal processing relative to category norm
        • Third-party certification (USDA Organic, Certified Humane, Regenerative Organic Certified)
        Red Flags
        • Long ingredient lists dominated by industrial additives, gums, and stabilizers with no functional necessity
        • Seed oils as a primary fat source when a more stable alternative exists in-category

        • What is the primary fat source, and is it stable and minimally processed?

        • Is there third-party certification behind any sourcing claim?
        Tradeoffs

        Convenience/packaged food vs. whole-food ideal: Packaged food is evaluated on its own curve relative to other packaged options, not penalized for not being unprocessed.

        Organic vs. local: When these conflict, both documented as valid, different priorities rather than one being universally superior.

        Cost vs. quality: Genuinely acknowledged as real; Tier 3 exists specifically for this.

        Tier Definitions

        Tier 1: Short, whole-food ingredient list, disclosed and verified sourcing, no red-flag additives, minimal processing for its category.

        Tier 2: Mostly clean formulation with one or two minor compromises and no major red flags.

        Tier 3: Avoids the most significant red flags but may have less sourcing transparency or a longer ingredient list.

        Exceptions
        • Medically necessary or allergen-driven formulations evaluated with that constraint disclosed.
        • Emergency/shelf-stable food preparedness held to a different standard than daily-consumption food.
        Future Research Notes
        • Needs a clear internal position on contested topics (seed oils, saturated fat) — acknowledging genuine scientific debate rather than presenting one side as settled.
        • Regenerative agriculture certification is still maturing; needs periodic review.

        What We're Optimizing For
        • Natural fiber content in direct-contact textiles
        • Absence of flame-retardant and off-gassing chemical treatments
        • Certified material sourcing
        • Realistic durability and lifespan
        Green Flags
        • GOTS-certified organic cotton, linen, or verified-process bamboo-derived sheets
        • GOLS-certified organic latex, or untreated natural wool/cotton fill
        • CertiPUR-US or GREENGUARD Gold certification where foam is used
        • Wool as a natural, non-chemical flame-retardant layer
        Red Flags
        • Undisclosed flame-retardant chemistry

        • If foam or latex is used, is it certified for low emissions?

        • What is the realistic expected lifespan before material breakdown?

        Natural latex firmness/cost vs. memory foam contouring: A genuine preference and performance tradeoff — both can reach Tier 1/2 depending on certification.

        Wool flame-barrier vs. synthetic-free claims: Some "natural" mattresses use wool specifically as flame protection — this is the green-flag approach, not a compromise.

        Tier Definitions

        Tier 1: Certified organic/natural fiber content, certified low-emission foam/latex if used, natural flame-resistance approach, full material disclosure.

        Tier 2: Natural-fiber-dominant with a minor synthetic or uncertified component, or certified foam without full organic fiber certification elsewhere.

        Tier 3: Avoids undisclosed chemical flame retardants and has basic certification but may be predominantly synthetic fiber.

        Exceptions
        • Medical/adjustable bed bases and specialty support surfaces evaluated on functional and safety merit separately.
        • Children's bedding held to a stricter, not looser, version of this standard.
        Future Research Notes
        • Flame-retardant regulation varies by state; needs periodic review.
        • Bamboo fiber processing methods vary widely (mechanical vs. chemical/viscose) with very different safety profiles.

        • Industrial/commercial-strength products are out of scope for this consumer standard.
        Future Research Notes
        • Indoor air quality research on VOC off-gassing is active and evolving.
        • EPA Safer Choice certification coverage is not universal across all subcategories yet.

        Indoor air is where most people spend the majority of their time, and problems are largely invisible without measurement. The focus is separating genuinely effective, tested equipment from marketing-driven claims that don't correspond to measurable performance.

        What We're Optimizing For
        • Verified filtration performance (CADR, true HEPA vs. "HEPA-type")
        • Absence of ozone-generating technology
        • Appropriately sized equipment for its intended space
        • Realistic, disclosed filter replacement cost and schedule
        Green Flags
        • True HEPA filtration with a disclosed, independently verified CADR rating
        • No ionization or ozone-generation technology, or independently verified to produce no measurable ozone
        • Transparent, reasonably priced replacement filter availability
        Red Flags
        • "Ionic" or "ozone" purifiers with no independent ozone-output testing
        • "HEPA-type"/"HEPA-like" language (not true HEPA)
        • No disclosed CADR rating or unsupported square-footage claims
        • Proprietary, expensive, hard-to-source filters relative to unit price

        • Is the filtration true HEPA, and is CADR independently verified?
        • Does the unit generate ozone or ions, and is output independently tested?
        • Is the unit appropriately sized for its marketed use case?
        • Are replacement filters reasonably available and priced?
        • Is there independent, non-brand-published performance data?
        Tradeoffs

        Noise vs. filtration power: Higher CADR often means more fan noise — disclosed per product, not folded into the safety score.

        Whole-home ventilation vs. portable purifiers: Different investment levels and different problems solved — evaluated on their own merits.

        Tier Definitions

        Tier 1: True HEPA with independently verified CADR, no ozone generation, appropriately sized, accessible replacement filters.

        Tier 2: True HEPA with brand-reported CADR, or a strong unit with a moderately priced/available filter ecosystem.

        Tier 3: Decent filtration performance without full independent verification, and no ozone-generation red flag.

        Exceptions
        • Medical-grade air filtration for specific respiratory conditions evaluated against clinical standards.
        • Industrial/commercial HVAC filtration is out of scope.
        Future Research Notes
        • Needs a clear, written position on ionization technology generally.
        • Smart/connected purifiers with sensor-based operation may need their own evaluation criteria.

        • Spectral accuracy matching a product's stated purpose

        Red light therapy cost vs. clinical-grade evidence: Higher-cost devices with disclosed, studied wavelength and irradiance data are preferred over inexpensive devices making the same claims without transparency.

        Tier Definitions

        Tier 1: Independently measured and disclosed spectral/EMF data, claims matched to actual evidence strength, clinically relevant wavelength ranges where applicable.

        Tier 2: Reasonable, mostly evidence-aligned claims with brand-reported (not independently verified) spectral data.

        Tier 3: Functional product serving the basic use case without red-flag marketing overreach, even without extensive independent testing.

        Exceptions
        • Devices marketed and regulated as medical devices (e.g., FDA-cleared light therapy) evaluated against their regulatory clearance.
        Future Research Notes
        • This category needs more frequent review than more settled ones, given how actively research is developing.
        • EMF health effects at consumer exposure levels remain contested; standard should track mainstream regulatory consensus.

        Tradeoffs

        Cost of third-party-tested products vs. budget options: Independent testing meaningfully raises cost — Tier 3 exists for reputable-but-untested products rather than excluding budget options entirely.

        Single-ingredient vs. formulated blends: Blends aren't disqualified but require full dose disclosure to reach Tier 1.

        Tier Definitions

        Tier 1: Third-party tested for label accuracy and contaminants, fully disclosed dosing, well-studied bioavailable ingredient forms.

        Tier 2: Third-party tested for label accuracy but not full contaminant panel, or fully disclosed dosing from an established, reputable manufacturer without independent verification.

        Tier 3: No independent testing, but full dosing disclosure and no red-flag ingredient forms.

        Exceptions
        • Practitioner-dispensed or prescription-adjacent supplements evaluated with that context noted, not held to a stricter consumer-retail standard.
        Future Research Notes
        • FDA regulatory changes to supplement oversight should be tracked closely.
        • Emerging ingredient categories (novel adaptogens, mushroom extracts) need a clear way to flag "promising but early evidence" versus well-established ingredients.
